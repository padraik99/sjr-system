01 — Diagnosis

Clinical Map

Primary Lesion

L4–L5 left foraminal narrowing compressing the L5 nerve root. MRI confirmed. Left lower lumbar facet joint dysfunction and piriformis syndrome are downstream of this, not independent problems.

Why the Calf Bands & Tingling Persist

L5 nerve root innervates the tibialis anterior, extensor hallucis longus, and contributes to the peroneal group. "Hair around the big toe," lateral foot tingling, and achy calf bands are all classic L5 distribution — consistent with the lesion, not a new problem. The fact that they worsen post-loading and improve with rest confirms mechanical compression as the primary driver, not central sensitization. This is good news: the epidural has a clear mechanical target.

What's Actually Improving

Glute pain reduction post-piriformis injection = the piriformis was genuinely contributing, not just co-morbid. Calf band intensity declining = the nervous system is slowly decompressing. Morning pain plateau at 2/10 is not a ceiling — it reflects the remaining mechanical compression that the epidural should address directly.

The One Real Flag

Full-depth bodyweight squats that reproduce tingling — even transiently, even fading — means loading a sensitized nerve root at end range. Pre-epidural, this is unnecessary provocation. Modify immediately. The trap bar fills this gap cleanly.

What the Epidural Will (and Won't) Do

Corticosteroid at L4–L5 reduces local inflammation and nerve sleeve edema. This is what's currently suppressing full glute recruitment — L5 innervates gluteus medius, and a compressed L5 root means neurogenic inhibition regardless of how hard you try to fire it. Post-injection, expect a recruitment window of 2–6 weeks where muscles that have been neurogenically suppressed suddenly become trainable. That window is where the biggest gains happen. The epidural does not fix the structural narrowing — but it reduces the neurological noise enough that your body can compensate effectively and rebuild.

03 — Immediate Add

Cycling Protocol

Start This Week — Stationary Bike at the Gym

This is not cardio. This is nerve decompression with a cardiovascular side effect. In hip flexion, the L4–L5 foramen opens measurably — the same reason backward jogging feels better than forward jogging. The stationary bike applies the same principle with zero joint impact and sustained duration. You have been doing this instinctively. Now formalize it.

ParameterPrescriptionRationale
Duration 15–20 min Enough for sustained foraminal opening without fatigue-driven form loss
Resistance Low — conversational pace Training the nerve environment, not the cardiovascular system. Yet.
Position Upright to slightly forward — NOT reclined Reclined bike reduces hip flexion angle, defeats the purpose
Frequency 3–4x/week, on heavier lower body days Counteracts compressive load from strength work same session
Timing Post-strength OR standalone Post-work: active decompression. Standalone: pure nerve mobility session
Monitor Calf bands during and 1hr after Should not worsen. If they do: reduce resistance and duration, not frequency
Progression Gate

Once 20 min at low resistance is trivially easy AND does not aggravate calf bands: increase to 30 min OR add one resistance level. Not both simultaneously. Road bike resumes when you feel ready — same protocol applies initially.

Bench, rows, shoulders are earning their keep and preserving your sanity. One flag only: if any pressing movement causes hard breath-holding (Valsalva), back off the load. Intra-abdominal pressure spikes transiently increase disc pressure. Not a reason to stop pressing — a reason to keep weight in a range where you can breathe through the rep.

06 — Untapped Asset

Trap Bar Protocol

The trap bar is your most important underused tool. It allows hip hinge loading with centered load, neutral spine, and adjustable start height — the only bilateral lower body compound movement that is genuinely safe pre-epidural when done correctly.

Why Trap Bar Over Conventional Deadlift

A conventional bar pulls load anterior to your center of mass, requiring lumbar extension to clear — that extension closes the posterior foramen. The trap bar centers load directly under your hips. Biomechanically it sits between a squat and a deadlift, without the full knee flexion demand of a squat. For L4–L5 foraminal compression, this is the right tool for this moment.

StageSetupLoadGate
1 — Now Elevated start (plates/blocks, bar at mid-shin or above). High handles. Empty bar or 25s — groove the pattern Zero tingling during or after 3 consecutive sessions
2 — Bridge Same elevated position. High handles. Add 10–20 lbs/session until mild effort Comfortable 5×5 at moderate load, calf bands unchanged post-session
3 — Post-Epidural Begin lowering start height incrementally. Still high handles. Reset to 70% of Stage 2 peak and rebuild Full glute recruitment visibly improved (you'll feel the difference)
4 — Return Phase Near-floor pull if symptom-free. Low handles option. Build toward meaningful load for sport prep 60 min walk post-session with no symptom spike
Non-Negotiable Form Cues — Pre-Epidural

1. Breath in before the pull, exhale through the lift — no Valsalva at this stage. 2. If you feel lumbar rounding at the start position, raise the bar. The start position governs everything. 3. Any left-leg tingling during the lift = stop that set. Note the load and stay below it next session. 4. Push the floor away — don't think about pulling with your back.

08 — Progression

Advancement Gates

These govern phase transitions. The calendar is a suggestion. These are the actual rules.

1
Trap Bar Entry
Begin at elevated height, light load. Gate to progress: zero tingling during or within 1hr of 3 consecutive sessions. If triggered, raise start height and reduce load — don't stop.
2
Cycling Progression
20 min at low resistance without calf band aggravation for 2 weeks. Then: add 10 min OR one resistance level. Not both simultaneously.
3
Post-Epidural Unlock
2 weeks post-injection before meaningful load increase. The injection itself creates local inflammation that needs to settle. Light walking, cycling, upper body only for 2 weeks. Then exploit the window aggressively.
4
Forward Jogging
Morning pain consistently ≤1/10 for 2 weeks. Walking 60 min with zero symptom spike. Post-epidural glute recruitment visibly improved. Start with 30s forward jog intervals — not a run.
5
King of the Court
30 min continuous jogging without symptom spike. Lateral shuffle at 75% effort pain-free. Ability to decelerate from sprint without bracing. This is the gate — not a date, not a season.
