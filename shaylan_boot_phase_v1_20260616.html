Why the legs don't rest

Training the LEFT leg protects the RIGHT one

This isn't busywork — it's cross-education.

Train one leg hard and the untrained leg gets stronger too — roughly 10–15% of the trained side's gain. The effect is neural: you're training the shared wiring in the spinal cord and motor cortex, not just the muscle. Picture a pianist with one arm in a cast — if the brain keeps rehearsing the piece through the free hand, the silenced hand forgets the music slower and relearns it faster when the cast comes off.

Studies that immobilized one limb and trained the other show the casted side loses less strength and muscle — and in a recent study of women in slings, it also recovered faster once the sling came off (Farthing 2009; Carr 2025). So every hard left-leg set buys back strength on the right and a faster ramp in September. Two rules to make it count: train heavy with slow, controlled lowering (the eccentric — the down phase — transfers best), and mirror the patterns the right leg needs back (squat / hinge / knee lines), not random machines.

The dose (expert consensus, Manca 2021): 3 sessions/week of heavy, eccentric-biased work for 4–6 weeks — which is essentially your whole boot window, so the timing lines up perfectly. The panel specifically endorsed this for orthopedic and sports injuries, found no reason to program it differently for women, and noted that any left/right imbalance it creates is a worthwhile trade for protecting the hurt side. They also flag one free booster worth trying: a mirror — set it along your midline so the reflection of your working left leg appears where the right one would be, and your brain "watches" the injured leg move strong while you train.

The one catch: tension counts, not just body weight

Your instinct here is correct. The navicular isn't only loaded by standing on it. The tibialis posterior — the only tendon that attaches to the navicular — pulls directly on it, and the research on navicular stress fractures specifically names that posterior-tibial pull, alongside compression, as what concentrates stress at the bone's poorly-supplied center. So indirect tension can load a healing navicular even with zero weight through the foot.

That's why the rule isn't "no standing" — it's the right foot and ankle stay completely passive. Knee and hip work with a limp, relaxed foot don't pull on the navicular and are fine. But anything that makes the foot or ankle do work — pointing the toe against a band, turning the sole inward (inversion), gripping with the toes, actively holding the arch — tensions the navicular and stays out until the care team clears it, body weight or not.

Blood-flow restriction (BFR) — the muscle-saver worth asking about ask PT

This is the one genuinely new high-ROI tool. A cuff that partially limits blood flow lets a light load — or even no load at all — produce a heavy-training signal in the muscle. Two uses for you:

Right (immobilized) thigh: BFR — on its own, or paired with the quad NMES above — attenuates the muscle and strength loss of disuse, with no joint motion and zero foot load (reviewed protocol: ~5 × 5 min cuffed / 3 min free, twice daily; BFR+EMS, Zhao 2020 / Hughes review). Left leg: low-load BFR lets the good leg chase a big stimulus without grinding heavy iron every session.

BFR is now standard in ACL rehab, so it also rehearses a tool you'll likely use at Berkeley. You already have the occlusion cuffs, so the only open question is the pressure (limb-occlusion %) and rep scheme — get that from the PT. Use the real cuffs, never a band knotted around the leg (uncontrolled pressure).

Swimming — cleared, with the legs strapped out of it

The pool was previously ruled out; the written clearance puts it back on the table. It's excellent NWB conditioning and a mental reset. The catch is the usual tendon: a flutter or dolphin kick is repeated ankle plantarflexion, which pulls tibialis posterior on the navicular.

So make kicking physically impossible, don't leave it to willpower: a pull buoy between the thighs PLUS an ankle strap/band binding the ankles together — the standard no-kick rig lap swimmers use to isolate the pull. Arms and lats do all the work; both legs trail dead, the right foot never drives. Confirm with the PT before it's a staple — the clearance is new and the navicular is high-risk.

🦴 Fuel the bone

Good news: the RED-S screen came back clear.

A healing bone is hungry — this is a phase to eat enough, not less, even though training volume drops. Bone repair runs on adequate energy plus calcium, vitamin D, and protein. Keep meals regular and protein steady across the day. You've been referred to a sports dietitian for the D1 transition — bring the navicular up so they can dial in calcium / vitamin D specifically for bone healing. Trust their numbers over anything generic here.

NWB non-negotiables

  • Crutches every step. No "quick hops" on the boot — one bad load can cost weeks on a high-risk bone.
  • Protect the good side. Crutching overloads your hands, shoulders, and left leg — the upper work doubles as armor against compensation strain.
  • Move daily. Even on off days: arm bike, mobility, get blood moving. Stillness is the enemy of morale, not of healing.
  • Sleep is rehab. Bone remodels overnight — protect 8+ hours.
  • Any new foot pain at rest or sharp pain → stop and tell the care team. Not a "push through" phase.
