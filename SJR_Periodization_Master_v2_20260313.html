01 — Foundation

Periodization Principles

Periodization is not a schedule. It is a biological argument — an acknowledgment that the human body cannot express all qualities simultaneously, that adaptation requires sequenced stress and recovery, and that peak performance on a specific date requires working backward from that date with precision. The calendar serves the physiology, not the other way around.

For this family, three additional constraints apply that conventional periodization literature rarely addresses simultaneously: active injury management during a performance build (Cadence), neurological recovery intersecting with sport-specific preparation (Patrick), and environment transition mid-season (Shaylan, Berkeley). The templates below absorb these constraints rather than ignoring them.

01
Specificity Over Time
Training progressively narrows toward the demands of the target event. GPP is broad. SPP is focused. Competition prep is precise. Schexnayder: every training decision should be defensible against the question — does this make the athlete better at the event?
02
Supercompensation
Tissue adapts during recovery, not during loading. The load is the stimulus; the rest is the adaptation. Baar: collagen synthesis peaks 6 hours post-load. The taper before competition is not lost training — it is the final adaptation window.
03
CNS Budget
The nervous system has a finite recovery capacity. Natera: maximal intensity work (sprints, jumps, heavy lifts) draws from the same CNS budget. Track volume and weight room volume are not separate — they compete. Account for both.
04
Tissue Lag
Tendons, fascia, and cartilage adapt 8–12 weeks behind muscle. Baar: you can train muscle faster than tendon tolerates. Injury is the body collecting a debt the athlete didn't know they owed. Load progression must respect tissue timelines, not ambition timelines.
05
Reversibility
Detraining begins within 2 weeks of inactivity for neuromuscular qualities; cardiovascular fitness degrades faster. The goal during injury or transition is maintenance, not improvement. Holding 80% of peak capacity costs far less than rebuilding from 40%.
06
Individual Response
Templates are starting points. Autoregulation — adjusting session load based on daily readiness — is not weakness, it is sophistication. McGill: pain is data. Nerve symptom changes are data. The template bends to the athlete, not the athlete to the template.

05 — Athlete Scenario

Shaylan — Nationals to Berkeley GPP

Shaylan · 100m · 200m · Long Jump · MJC → UC Berkeley Fall 2026
Post-Nationals Transition + Berkeley Entry GPP
Now — March 12 → Berkeley start ~August 2026 · 19 weeks · ISA: Narrow · Active PFPS left knee
Competition Now → Nationals
~12 weeks
Stay the course with PFPS modification. Left knee is the penultimate step foot — this is the rate-limiting mechanical issue, not just a pain management problem. Continue training through with isometric analgesia pre-session (Rio protocol: 5×45s wall sit). The penultimate step mechanics work documented in her individual protocol is active training, not remedial work. Every drill session is simultaneously fixing the PFPS mechanism and improving the jump. Keep PFPS loading below 3/10 pain during activity — above that, reduce session volume, not intensity.
Taper ~10 days pre-Nationals
Standard taper with PFPS caveat: do not unload the knee completely. The patellar tendon and retropatellar cartilage need maintained load to arrive at competition in a responsive state, not a cold state. 2×10 min isometric wall sit maintained through taper week. Volume drops 50%, intensity maintained. One full approach LJ two days before competition — no more.
Transition Post-Nationals
2–3 weeks
Decompress, not collapse. Travel window is here. Unstructured movement — swimming, walking, light cycling. No track work. PFPS tissue gets genuine rest for possibly the first time since the episode began. Collagen protocol continues — this is the window where the tissue can remodel without the competition load signal. Sleep prioritized. Mental reset from competition mode. This is not wasted time; it is the biological reason the GPP block that follows will be productive.
GPP 4 weeks pre-Berkeley
Berkeley-agnostic GPP with one design constraint: establish the tissue baseline and movement quality that allows Berkeley's coaching staff to build on a solid foundation rather than inheriting an injury problem. Priorities in order: (1) PFPS resolved or stable — the Berkeley training volume will be higher than MJC, and arriving with an unresolved patellar issue into elevated load is a structural risk. (2) Dorsiflexion restored — the upstream driver of the lateral patellar tracking. (3) Penultimate mechanics consolidated — so Berkeley coaches see a mechanically sound jumper, not one with a compensation pattern baked in. GPP template applies. Max sprint intensity capped at 80%.
Berkeley Entry Gate
PFPS ≤1/10 on stairs for 2 consecutive weeks. Full dorsiflexion range symmetrical bilateral. Penultimate COM lowering visible on video (self-assessed). These are the conditions under which Berkeley's staff inherits a healthy athlete, not a project.
06 — Athlete Scenario

Cadence — Commonwealth Games Peak · Glasgow July 23

Cadence · 100m · 200m · Long Jump · Cal Poly · Week 11 PF load intolerance
Return-to-Compete → Commonwealth Peak
March 12 → July 23 · 19 weeks · ISA: Narrow · PF neural sensitivity active · Coach-managed
Return Now → Week 6
6 weeks
The gate that governs everything else. Neural desensitization is the primary target — not fitness, not strength, not speed. The mechanical chain (PF guarding → calcaneal restriction → subtalar locked → tib post overload → medial shin) unwinds in that order. Track A daily. Collagen window before every session. First meet in 1.5 weeks is one data point only — use it as a readiness assessment, not a performance target. Coach is managing this correctly; the role here is to support the tissue environment, not push the timeline.
SPP Weeks 7–15
9 weeks
Conditional on Week 6 gate. If PF load tolerance is established and neural sensitivity has reduced, begin SPP template with modified calf/ankle loading protocol. Sprint volume builds progressively — Schexnayder short-to-long model: acceleration work before speed endurance. LJ approach volume increases weekly. The shin activation under circuit loading at Week 11 (reported) is expected and not alarming — it will resolve as load tolerance improves. Monitor weekly: if shin pain climbs above 3/10 during session, that week is a maintenance week, not an SPP week.
Nationals ~Week 13
Compete
Nationals is a qualifying performance AND a readiness test for Glasgow. The ideal outcome is a clean, symptom-free competition that demonstrates the tissue holds under max-effort conditions. A slower time with zero symptoms is a better Glasgow signal than a personal best followed by a flare. Coach and athlete should align on this framing pre-competition.
Peak Weeks 14–15
Pre-taper
Highest quality sprint and jump work of the cycle. Volume begins to drop, intensity at absolute peak. Full approach LJ 3×/week maximum. Flying sprint work. Baar contrast warmup protocol on every session. This is the window where the competition peak is set — the taper preserves it, it doesn't create it.
Taper Weeks 16–17
July 9–22
PF-modified taper — do not unload completely. Standard taper error: removing all load from a recovering PF athlete causes the tissue to lose the stiffness that makes it functional at competition. Maintain 2×15 single-leg calf raise daily through taper. One full approach LJ at Days 7 and 3 pre-competition. Volume −50%, intensity maintained. No new interventions — massage only from practitioners familiar with her history.
Glasgow Honest Gate
Week 6: Full training session (sprint + jump + weight room) with PF ≤2/10 during and ≤1/10 next morning. If not cleared by Week 6, Glasgow timeline compresses — the sharpening block shortens and peak form is harder to guarantee. Not impossible, but honest. This gate determines whether Glasgow is a target or a stretch goal.

Patrick · 59 · L4-L5 L5 nerve root compression · Week 21 · Pre-epidural
Bridge → Unlock → Return-to-Court
March 12 → Summer 2026 · Gate-governed · ISA: Moderate-Wide · 6–8 weeks post-clearance for full court prep
Bridge Now → Epidural
~4 weeks
Bridge template active. Cycling added this week. Trap bar entry at elevated start. Full-depth squat replaced with box squat. The training goal here is explicit: arrive at the epidural in the best possible neuromuscular state — glutes as activated as the nerve allows, posterior chain maintained, no new provocations. See Patrick individual protocol for full detail.
Settle Post-ESI · 2 weeks
Two weeks of deliberate restraint. The epidural creates local inflammation at the injection site before it reduces nerve sleeve edema. Training hard in this window is counterproductive — it adds systemic inflammatory load to a tissue trying to settle. Light walking, cycling, upper body maintenance only. This is not lost time; it is the price of the unlock that follows.
Unlock/GPP Weeks 3–8
post-ESI
The most important training block of the year. Neurogenic inhibition of gluteus medius lifts — possibly completely, possibly partially. The muscles that have been suppressed by L5 compression suddenly become trainable. Trap bar progression accelerates. Single-leg glute bridge becomes single-leg hip thrust. Clamshells become lateral band walks under load. Cycling becomes intervals. Forward jog introduced at Gate 4 criteria. This is a GPP block in structure but feels like early SPP because the nervous system is releasing previously unavailable capacity. Exploit it systematically.
RTS SPP Weeks 9–14
post-ESI
Return-to-sport specificity. Lateral deceleration is the first true King of the Court demand. Change of direction, reactive stopping, single-leg landing from a lateral step — these are neurologically expensive patterns that require 6–8 weeks of deliberate training before they're safe at competitive intensity. A-march and A-skip introduced as movement quality work, not sprint preparation. Jogging progresses from 30s intervals to 10-minute continuous runs. Energy system work on the bike becomes genuinely cardiovascular.
KotC Prep Weeks 15–22
post-ESI
Court-specific conditioning. 30+ league volleyball has specific demands that differ from pure fitness: explosive vertical, rapid lateral shuffle, repeated deceleration, upper body power under fatigue. The 6–8 week estimate for full prep post-clearance is accurate — this is where that time is spent. Trap bar loaded meaningfully. Sprint work at 70–80% with direction changes. The goal is not peak athletic performance; it is resilient participation — the ability to play a full King of the Court session, recover normally, and play again the next week.
King of the Court Gate
30 min continuous jog without symptom spike. Lateral shuffle at 75% effort pain-free. Ability to decelerate from a sprint without bracing the left side. Morning pain ≤1/10 consistently. These gates are not about fitness — they are about nervous system trust in the left leg.
08 — Acute:Chronic

Load Management Across the Family

The single most common cause of injury in well-intentioned athletes is a rapid spike in training load — the acute:chronic workload ratio problem. The body can absorb roughly a 10% week-on-week increase in load before tissue injury risk increases meaningfully. This applies to all three athletes, scaled by current capacity.

Athlete Current Load Tolerance Max Weekly Increase Deload Frequency Primary Limiter
Shaylan Moderate — PFPS active, competition load 10% sprint volume; 15% weight room Every 3 weeks Left knee patellar load
Cadence Low–Moderate — return to training, Week 11 10% any load increase Every 2–3 weeks PF neural sensitivity
Patrick Low currently — nerve compression, pre-epidural 5–10% lower body; 15% upper body Every 3 weeks L5 nerve root tolerance
Universal Rule
When any of the primary symptom indicators spike — PFPS pain above 3/10, PF pain above 3/10, calf bands or tingling above baseline — that week's load increase is cancelled and the week becomes a maintenance week. The 10% rule is a ceiling, not a target. Autoregulation beats the template every time.
